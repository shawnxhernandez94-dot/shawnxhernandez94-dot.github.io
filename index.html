About

I'm a Philosophy Ph.D. candidate at the University of Pennsylvania, where I work at the intersection of ethics, political philosophy, and the moral questions that shape how we live together.

Alongside my research, I work as a lecturer designing philosophy curriculum for secondary and undergraduate students. My work is driven by the conviction that philosophy should be rigorous, accessible, and genuinely useful — not just true in the seminar, but illuminating in the world.

Research

My dissertation investigates questions of justice and obligation — how the circumstances of birth, belonging, and social position shape what we owe one another. I am especially interested in the moral weight we assign to accidents of origin: birthplace, citizenship, family.

work on borders & belonging
The Accident of Birthplace (in progress) — asks whether morally arbitrary facts about origin can ground legitimate claims of exclusion, and what Esperanza's story reveals about the ethics of borders.
work on obligation & moral standing
What We Owe Across Borders (draft) — examines the relationship between political membership and moral obligation in an era of displacement and migration.
work on technology & ethics
Digital Obligations (in progress) — explores the moral dimensions of networked life and what obligations arise in cybersecurity and digital communities.
Teaching

I design curriculum that brings ethical and political philosophy into conversation with the real dilemmas students encounter. I am currently developing a lesson package centered on the question:

Should the accident of Esperanza's birthplace affect what she is owed? — using fiction as a vehicle for rigorous philosophical inquiry with 9th–10th grade students.

My public-facing work explores philosophy's relationship to technology, digital ethics, and the moral landscape of contemporary life.
